Week 1: Overview: Your Professional Identity & How We Are the Same

What are we doing in this module? Who are you now and where are we going? How are we the same?

This module, the second of four Creative Industries Units (CIU), develops learnings skills by integrating practice-based and knowledge-based skills with the study of the Creative Industries. Students will be mentored to develop a deeper understanding of their identities as creative media practitioners within the wider creative media industry and with the goal of becoming connected, self-directed practitioners.

So what does that mean? Think about what you’re here to do. You’re here to get better at making films, games, websites, graphics, code, audio engineering and so on. Most of your classes are geared towards the production skills needed to develop the craft (and art) of your creative practice. But there is something else you will need too…

Let’s say you make a game. Once that is done, then you need to figure out how to submit it to iTunes or Steam, and then to promote it so people know about it.

Or you make a film. Where are you going to find your crew? How will you get it to your audience?

You may have all the web design skills, but if you don’t know where the meetups are or what clients are looking for, how will you be paid to make websites?

The confronting truth about working in the Creative Industries is that the strength of your work alone does not guarantee you a career. I’ll bet you can think of an example of a figure from your discipline who was unheralded in their own time, but who has posthumously (after death) been celebrated as a great artist? We’re still discovering such people. A great recent discovery is Connie Converse. Haven’t heard of her? No one had until very recently.

How a 90-Year-Old Missing Person Became a Hit on Spotify
Dan Dzula first heard Connie Converse's music on NPR in 2004. It was an amateur recording, taken in someone's kitchen…priceonomics.com

As the article above relates, Converse was a singer-songwriter in New York in the 1950s:

Converse appears to have been a forerunner to the present-day American Songwriter. Her music touched upon her own experience as an intelligent and creative young woman, trying to find love and make a place for herself in mid-Century America. Though many of her songs are humorous, the characters in them are lonely, unfulfilled, or somehow misfit to society’s norms.

We love Converse today, her hipster aesthetic and progressive, melancholic sentiment jibe with life in the early 21st century. She now has a vinyl record, a Bandcamp profile, and is much written about. But this success alluded her in life. She went unsigned while a few short years later the likes of Bob Dylan and Joan Baez would emerge as major recording artists. Why is this? We can think of a few reasons…

  • Maybe there wasn’t an audience for this kind of music yet?
  • Maybe the content of her music—including songs about hooking up with men in bars—was seen as too risque for the “mass media” channels that people accessed music through at the time? (television, radio)
  • Maybe the gatekeepers of the live performance and recording industry (record company executives, club owners and promoters), who were likely to be older men, didn’t think much of her?
  • Maybe she had a mental illness (like anxiety or depression) and this made it hard for her to “put herself out there” as a performer?

Fortunately both the media and society have changed since the 1950s. These changes allow us to build an audience, promote ourselves, and sell our work in ways that were impossible back then. This is not to say that these sorts of issues no longer exist, but that we have new means available to us to work around these blockages.

This module is about everything else you need to think about—on top of perfecting your creative work—to have a career; it’s about the business of being creative. It isn’t too early either. Do you want to get to the end of your degree with a portfolio and BEGIN the process of meeting industry professionals and building your online profile? (hint: you don’t). These things take years, so start today. Use this time to experiment with how you do these things, and build on them so once you have finished here you’re hitting the ground running.

How Are We the Same?

What do animators, audio engineers, game developers, filmmakers, web developers, and graphic artists share in common?

A place to start may be how we are seen by others. How are “creatives” depicted in entertainment?

In her study of new media workers, Rosalind Gill speaks of the ways new media workers are depicted. We are all creatives who work with new media, and are often written about and represented in media as follows:

- working in a trendy warehouse setting in the cultural quarter of a city
- a group of young people coded as“diverse” (male, female, black, white, gay, straight)
- “creative” (untidy, chaotic, obsessive)
- working long and unusual hours (e.g.getting up at lunchtime and then working through the night)
- and relate to each other in a casual and informal manner
‘Life is a pitch: Managing the self in new media work’
‘Life is a pitch: Managing the self in new media work’www.academia.edu

Though the videos above are satires that use stereotypes, the reason we squirm when we watch them is that there is more than a kernel of truth in each of them. They depict traits (positive and negative) that we share in the creative industries. Gill identifies 10 traits of work in the creative industries that feature across disciplines:

Entrepreneurism

Entrepreneurism is a characteristic of many who work in new media. What is entrepreneurism? A startup company may come to mind, but it is more than that. It is a state of mind, an attitude, a habit. It entails seeing opportunities to create something new, and the urge to bring it into the world. Much like creating art in general.

I believe that entrepreneurs aren’t just building companies or executing business plans. They’re creating their own reality. They’re not in it for the money. They’re in it for the freedom. They’re told that they’re taking a risk. They know that it’s not risk free. They’re not doing it because they can. They’re doing it because there’s no way they can not. They simply can not imagine doing anything else. Each is doing The Only Thing That Matters. And so is everyone else. (Jerry R. Mitchell)

You may think that to be an entrepreneur you need to be running your own business. Not so. More and more companies are recognising that the urge to innovate is an asset. So instead of losing these employees, they’re encouraging them to come up with ideas within the company. There is a term for this: intrapreneurs.

An intrapreneur is someone who has an entrepreneurial streak in his or her DNA, but chooses to align his or her talents with a large organization in place of creating his or her own. […] Smart organizations will seek out individuals who like to invent, innovate and want to be on the front lines of change. These individuals can work independently but even more important can work seamlessly as part of an integrated team structure and also effectively embrace and embody the culture of the intrapreneur’s host organization. (David Armano, Forbes)
Move Over Entrepreneurs, Here Come The Intrapreneurs

But whether you’re an employee, a freelancer, or running your own company, there is another trait we all share.

Short Term, Precarious Work

Gill explains that precarious and intermittent work is endemic to life in new media, as with much other cultural work. In film, TV, graphics, web, games, audio, and animation, all work is project-based. This means we or our employers have income for a while and then it disappears for an unknown period of time. This up-and-down of income means we share a lifestyle that includes: taking on several jobs, taking on ‘pay the bills’ jobs, no holidays, taking on parallel assignments, and exhaustion.

So if you take on several jobs, then you have to learn how to switch between the demands of each project. You can’t wait for inspiration, and sometimes it means switching between starkly different projects. You’re not able to soak in the head-space of one, but have to learn how to switch gears between many.

We all know the ‘pay the bills’ idea. By definition, it means that you’re doing something you wouldn’t do if you had the money. The beauty of it, though, is that you can be exposed to really interesting ideas and client constraints that stretch you as a creative.

Low Pay

We hear a lot about the “single big hit” or “breakthrough project” (McRobbie, 2007). There are “sudden” hits like Napoleon Dynamite, Superbad, Minecraft, Angry Birds, Red Hot Chili Peppers, Ello, skeuomorphism, Ship Your Enemies Glitter, but as Gill explains, this is (of course) not common. Most people in new media work for low pay.

There are a few reasons for this. There is a lot of competition for projects. If we’re dealing with a client, then different teams may pitch low to try and get the job. There are also excess hours that you may work that go unpaid. Part of this culture can be the idea of a “sacrificial ethos” (Andrew Ross, 2000), were you do art for art’s sake. Next week we’ll be discussing how you can get around the intermittent and low pay problem.

Long Hours

Gill continues, talking about how creative work in new media is marked by very long hours — particularly among those who freelance or work in their own micro-enterprises. They can work between 60 and 80 hours per week. If you’re freelancing or running your own company, you can do this many hours (and it can be pleasant if you’re enjoying what you’re doing). But a skill you’ll need to develop is how to manage your time, and making sure you have a work-life balance. You’ll work better when you have time away from work.

Keeping Up

Compared to other creative fields (e.g. fine art, performance), new media work is an industry in which knowledge changes at an extraordinarily rapid pace, new software packages and standards proliferate and people experience intense pressure to ‘keep up’ and stay abreast of current developments. Aphra Kerr concurs. Kerr found common characteristics of all cultural industries, which include the need for constant up-skilling:

— youthful age profile — flexible working hours — the erasure of boundaries between work and play — the need for constant re- and up-skilling — a high degree of mobility amongst industry workers
The Business and Culture of Digital Games: Gamework and Gameplay

DIY Learning

Gill found that new media workers are generally highly educated, often to degree level and beyond. Because of the need to ‘keep up’ with the latest technologies, there is a greater reliance of learning informally from the net as well, including ‘learning by doing’.

Informality

Work environments in the creative industries often feature informal settings where there is a “work as play” ethos. So we often see companies that feature game rooms, TV, and freely available food and drink.

This informality flows all the way to how work is found. While there are formal processes to finding and advertising work, most work is found socially by “who you know”. Gill explains that the entire economy of work opportunities operates through contacts — people you meet at conferences, parties, drinks evenings, friends of friends, ex-colleagues, social media, etc. This is why networking is an important aspect of your creative career.

Exclusions and Inequalities

We have the same issues as other industries when it comes to exclusions and inequalities. They include gender, age, class, race and ethnicity and disability; access to work, and rate of pay. New issues that we deal with include those related to autonomy, flexibility and informality; and according to the earlier study, a smaller proportion of women have children relative to their peers outside of the creative industries.

…then imagine your life in 5 years…

…then 10 years.

Did you have trouble picturing exactly what you will be doing? There is a reason for that, and you’re not alone…

No Future

A common experience of people who work in new media is the inability to imagine their future. The factors cited above, such as intermittent work, contribute to this. As Gill explains, the “material conditions of radical uncertainty lead to an inability to project ahead into the future in a realistic or meaningful way”. On the one hand, new media workers fantasise about a life of extreme success with all its trappings, and on the other hand complete failure or even a void as new media workers could seemingly not articulate any sense of their future. Imagining your future is important. You can’t work towards something if you don’t know where you’re going. What we cover in this module will help you be able to imagine and plan for your career.

Love the Work

Finally, the stand out thing that all people who work in creative industries share: they LOVE their work. Indeed, a consistent finding of research on new media workers is the passion it inspires, and the profound affective ties people develop to their work.

“The creative industries are peopled by creative talents who themselves get pleasure and utility from what they do. They are ‘called to their art.”
“Staying Ahead”, Work Foundation Report, UK, 2007

“You’ve got to find what you love. And that is as true for your work as it is for your lovers. Your work is going to fill a large part of your life, and the only way to be truly satisfied is to do what you believe is great work. And the only way to do great work is to love what you do.”
Steve Jobs

In this great mini-documentary, you will see creatives from a range of disciplines talking about all the things we’ve covered here, along with the great love of what they do…the inspiring side of working in creative industries!

Keep your passion for your creative work in mind as you process all the problems and pitfalls we’ve raised here in this lecture. And remember, everything we talk about for the next 12 weeks will be geared towards finding solutions to these problems so that you have the best possible chance of not only doing great work, but being successful in every sense of the word.
